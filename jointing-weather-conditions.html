Jointing in Weather Conditions

How heat, humidity, rain and dust affect cable joints and terminations — and the go / no-go limits and controls that keep them dry, clean and reliable.

This page gives general Quality Team good-practice for cable jointing in adverse weather. It does not replace the manufacturer's installation instructions or the project specification — where they set stricter limits, always follow the stricter requirement.

General requirements

A cable joint or termination is only as good as the environment it is made in. Moisture, dust and temperature all affect how insulation surfaces bond and how adhesives and heat-shrink materials cure. The overriding principle is simple: insulation surfaces must be clean, dry and at a controlled temperature at the moment the joint is closed.

  • Provide a proper enclosure — a jointing tent, cabin or shelter — before any cable is cut or a joint is opened.
  • Keep prepared surfaces covered until the moment they are needed; never leave an open joint unattended.
  • Have all materials, tools and consumables staged and within their shelf life before starting.
  • Record the ambient conditions (temperature and humidity) on the pre-work checklist and again if conditions change.

Temperature & humidity

Both high and low temperatures matter. In high ambient heat, adhesives can slump and heat-shrink recovery is harder to control; when it is cold, adhesives may not flow and cure times extend. Relative humidity drives condensation onto cool insulation surfaces, which is one of the most common causes of joint failure.

  • Follow the accessory manufacturer's stated temperature window for the material being installed.
  • Watch for condensation: if surfaces are cooler than the surrounding air, moisture can form even when it is not raining.
  • Use approved warming methods to bring surfaces and materials into range — never use an uncontrolled open flame near prepared insulation.
  • Where humidity is high, use dehumidifying or warming measures inside the enclosure before and during jointing.

Rain & moisture control

No joint should be opened in the open air while rain is falling or expected. Water on an insulation surface, or trapped inside a joint, leads to partial discharge and early failure that may not show up until the cable is energised.

  • Fully enclose and, where needed, raise the working area clear of standing water and run-off.
  • Keep a dry-out and warming plan ready; if a surface gets wet, stop, dry and re-verify before continuing.
  • Protect cable ends and accessories from wind-driven rain during transport and staging, not just during jointing.

Dust & sandstorm conditions

Airborne dust and sand are a major local risk. Fine particles settle onto adhesive and insulation surfaces and prevent a clean bond, and they are easy to miss because the surface can still look dry.

  • Do not open joints during active sandstorms — wait for conditions to clear and clean down the enclosure first.
  • Seal the enclosure against dust ingress and clean surfaces immediately before application.
  • Keep accessory packaging closed until the last moment to avoid contamination.

High ambient temperature working

In sustained high heat, protect both the work and the people. Materials left in direct sun can exceed their handling temperature, and technicians tire faster, which affects workmanship.

  • Shade and, where possible, cool the materials store and the working area.
  • Plan jointing for cooler parts of the day where the programme allows.
  • Allow for heat-related breaks so that preparation and finishing are not rushed.

Do's & don'ts

✔ Do

  • Enclose and prepare before opening any joint.
  • Keep surfaces clean, dry and within temperature range.
  • Record conditions on the pre-work checklist.
  • Stop and re-verify if the weather changes.
  • Follow the manufacturer's instructions for the accessory.

✘ Don't

  • Open a joint in falling rain or an active sandstorm.
  • Close a joint over a damp or contaminated surface.
  • Rush cure times to keep the programme.
  • Leave prepared surfaces or open joints uncovered.
  • Use uncontrolled open flame near insulation.
